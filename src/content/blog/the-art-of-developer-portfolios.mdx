I have reviewed hundreds of developer portfolios while hiring for my team, and I have a confession: most of them are forgettable. Not because the developers lack skill, but because the portfolios all look the same and fail to communicate what makes each person unique.

## The Template Problem

Every third portfolio I see uses the same Next.js template with a dark theme, a hero that says "Hi, I'm [Name]", a grid of project cards, and a contact form. There is nothing wrong with this structure, but when everyone uses it, nobody stands out.

The fix is not to build something wildly experimental. It is to be intentional about every element on the page and make choices that reflect who you are as a developer.

## Show, Don't Tell

The most common mistake is telling visitors you are a "passionate developer" instead of showing them. Your portfolio itself is a project. If you claim to care about performance, your site better load fast. If you say you are detail-oriented, every pixel should be intentional.

Include real project case studies, not just screenshots. Explain the problem, your approach, the technical decisions you made, and the outcome. A hiring manager reads "Built a dashboard that reduced reporting time by 60%" very differently from "Built a dashboard with React."

## The Three Things That Matter

After seeing hundreds of portfolios, three things consistently make me want to learn more about a candidate:

**Genuine writing.** A blog section with two or three well-written posts about technical topics shows me how you think and communicate. This matters more than another project card.

**Thoughtful project selection.** Show three to four projects that demonstrate range and depth. A personal project, an open-source contribution, and something built at scale. Quality over quantity.

**Personality.** A "Currently" section showing what you are reading, listening to, or working on makes you human. A fun 404 page shows attention to detail. These small touches add up.

## Technical Decisions That Reflect Skill

Your portfolio's tech stack is itself a signal. If you are a frontend developer using a 500KB JavaScript framework to render a static page, that tells me something about your judgment. If your site is fast, accessible, and well-structured, that tells me something better.

Use semantic HTML. Implement proper SEO. Make sure it works on mobile. These are basics, but a surprising number of developer portfolios fail them.

## Keep It Updated

A portfolio from 2022 with no recent projects suggests you have moved on. Keep your site current. Update your "Currently" section. Add new blog posts. Swap in your latest work. A living portfolio is worth ten times more than a stale one.

Your portfolio is your handshake with the internet. Make it count.
